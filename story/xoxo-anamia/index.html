XOXO, Ana/Mia
By Abby Shure
3:08 p.m. Feb. 23 2010

    Ednos loves math. Illogical to the brink of destruction, numbers are her favorite game.

    She delights in the chemistry of the human body. She savors the wonder of muscles and tendons, but she finds herself smitten with bones. She loves the way they poke out of collarbones and wrists and knees and the way they are like arrows, jutting out of her back.

    She sees her world through half-full glasses of water to quiet her hungry belly.

    “I can get better,” she says. “I will do my best, and I will succeed. I will win.”

    And she will. She is well equipped. A paper and pencil, to write down the pertinent ins and outs, a calculator for totals, a scale for progress, shoes for the journey, a toothbrush to fix mistakes and a computer. She will live and wilt by the computer.

    Her friend, it sticks by her when she needs support. “XOXO, Ana/Mia,”she reads. She finds her inspiration in pictures and movies and quotes and jokes that tell her she is right. This is her battle to win with the computer’s encouragement, but the flashing monitor becomes her enemy on judgment day. When she is forced to tell all, she screams at the screen. Her fingers tremble to record her failures. She cannot lose enough. More water.

    Even Ana and Mia can only help her so much. “Tomorrow is another day,” they say.  ”You can fix yourself tomorrow.”

    She sets goals for every tomorrow, goals she cannot reach paired with promises she cannot keep.  This is her private war, fought and won in the battles of days, in the volleys of meals and in the single shots of morsels.

    No one else can know. She can’t let her mother see, her friends see, or even herself see what she is doing. It is her little secret, she thinks, and she loves it.

    The lies help her. She is better because she can lie her way out of guilt and out of the prying eyes that tell her she is fine. She isn’t fine. She is getting there, though, if only you won’t try to stop her.

    But something in her wants to be stopped. To be told that she is good enough, thin enough, pretty enough, finally enough. She needs this. It’s why she started in the first place. It’s one part attention, one part self-destruction, two parts willpower and boredom and pressure and an escape.

    It’s a difficult escape, but so worth it to wake up in the morning with a clear conscience and an empty stomach. It’s what she has always wanted.

    It makes her better. Better than her every other woman who cannot control herself, who feeds her soul with cakes and pies and chocolate. Ednos feeds a malnourished heart with promise.

    Her blood has become a slush of water and Diet Coke. Her head is light as she runs and twists and lifts and crunches on nothing but air and her legs and arms shake with the perfect exhaustion of burning it all off until she can’t feel anything anymore.  She is weak enough to fall into bed without dinner, the only ending perfect enough to fit the way her body feels when it’s empty.

    Her days pass by, full of the sick math she loves. Onetwothreefourfivesixseveneight glasses of water. 1500 1000 500 300 200 fast: One-two-three-four-five-six-seven-eight-nine-ten-eleven-twelve-thirteen-fourteen-fifteen-sixteen-seventeen-eighteen-nineteen-twenty-twenty-one-twenty-two-twenty-three-twenty-four hours without eating.

    She passes her first fast. She feasts, having worked hard for each crescent of juice and pulp that is her clementine dinner. A spinach leaf, a few pieces of popcorn (no butter, no salt). More water.

    “You totally look like an anorexic, except you, like, shouldn’t be in the hospital,” she hears. She laughs with pleasure. She smiles as she pushes the food around on her plate, enjoying the comfortable feel of hunger.

    One year later, she finds herself missing the Ednos she has left behind with her twisted view of her body. She finds pieces of discarded paper with ins and outs and sometimes writes her own to see if she can do it. Like the skin and muscle and fat she lost, the spirit of Ana is close as she stares at her curves in the mirror.

    Ednos is in every bite, every plate, every meal, every day as she goes to bed and wonders if dinner was worth it. Was it?

    More water, please.
