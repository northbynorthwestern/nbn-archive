Wrinkled warnings
By Abby Shure
2:52 p.m. May 26 2010

    You might increase your chances of getting wrinkles and cancer if you enter in one of these. Photo by mag3737 on Flickr, licensed under the Creative Commons.

    This winter, Weinberg freshman ToniAnn Trevino went tanning for “the first and last time” at L.A. Tan on Chicago Avenue. Trevino felt that she looked too “pasty,” she said, but decided to stop tanning after researching the major detriments of using indoor tanning beds.

    “I remembered that, oh yeah, cancer is a threat,” Trevino said. Although it “seemed like a quick fix at the time,” she didn’t want the cancer or any other problems that came with using the beds for an extended period.

    Trevino is one of many college women who try or regularly use tanning beds and are the most targeted by a new study published this month.

    Dr. June Robinson of Northwestern’s Feinberg School of Medicine published the report that offered perhaps the best way to stop women from using tanning beds: warning them about wrinkles.

    Robinson’s study explains that people are fundamentally concerned with looks and social perception, and the most effective way to prevent certain behaviors is to remind people of how it’s going to make them look. The tangible side effect of wrinkles is more powerful as a prevention tool than the possibility of skin cancer, where too many people simply think, “it won’t happen to me.”

    To keep your skin safe at Dillo Day this weekend:

    Take Robinson’s advice for lazy kids: use two coats of sunscreen. It takes about 4 ounces of sunscreen to cover your entire body, and since you’ll probably miss spots, just put on a regular amount, wait 10 to 20 minutes and put it on again. Repeat as needed throughout the day, especially if you’re going to sweat it off or jump in Lake Michigan.

    Sunscreen samples will be distributed for free from a Lakefill booth at Dillo Day by Plus Energy, a cancer-awareness student group.

    While Trevino chose not to tan because of the cancer risk — “you’d think the possibility of getting cancer would be enough,” she said — many women are more off put by the realization that they could have wrinkles by age 25.

    Communication sophomore Emily Marso tried tanning for the same reasons as Trevino — “I looked ghostly,” she said — but stopped for aesthetic reasons: too many freckles. While Marso wanted to look better in her dress for senior prom in high school, she didn’t want to freckle instead of tan, so she stopped heading to the booths.

    While the beds were “creepy and… like a futuristic death pod,” it was hard for Marso to see the consequences of tanning. She compared it to alcohol or overeating because “it’s harder to care about something without seeing immediate effects,” she said.

    While Marso had been warned by her mom many times that tanning could give her cancer, she stopped without really considering the harm to her health, a common result from Robinson’s study.

    Northwestern researchers played a key role in the study on ways to scare people into quitting the beds as a local hotbed of appearance-concerned college students.

    When the correlation between melanoma and indoor tanning first surfaced in the early ‘90s, Robinson took note. By 1995, she was interviewing North Beach lifeguards at SPAC to see why they were so concerned with being tan.

    Her findings, of course, seem obvious; the lifeguards thought that being tan made them look thinner, feel more attractive and not be seen as “wimps, nerds or boorkworms,” Robinson said.

    After her preliminary work at Northwestern, Robinson and her colleagues wrote pamphlets to get people to stop using indoor tanning beds and tested them at Penn State and Eastern Tennessee State University. The study, which tracked women who tanned regularly for six months after they were given their pamphlets, found that the reasons for tanning moved beyond attractiveness.

    It became more than just lifeguards on the shores of Lake Michigan trying to look hot; instead, Robinson found that many women had “social reasons” for tanning. Tanning had become a social activity, like shopping or getting coffee, where women met up over the winter.

    To get women to stop, the pamphlet suggested replacement activities and reminded women that they could have wrinkles in their mid-20s.

    Robinson explained away any potential “benefits” of indoor tanning in the winter — no, indoor tanning won’t help with seasonal affective disorder because it doesn’t have the UVB light that helps to trigger happiness from outside light, and no, it won’t help your Vitamin D deficiency. Instead, indoor tanning merely puts you at higher risk for skin cancer and ages your skin prematurely.

    After being informed of the risks of cancer, wrinkles and other nasty side effects of using tanning beds, most women in the study cut down their usage, or stopped all together.

    “In text lingo, the girls were finally like ‘OMG!’” said Robinson.
