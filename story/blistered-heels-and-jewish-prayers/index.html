Blistered heels and Jewish prayers
By Coco Keevan
3:14 p.m. Oct. 6 2009

    I feel as if so many people shift away from religion when they get to college. For me, arriving at Northwestern last fall translated into a haphazard religious discovery. Having been raised in a secular household with a Jewish mother and a Catholic father, I admit that I know very little about my faith, other than the traditional prayers for High Holidays. Following my senior year of high school, however, I traipsed to Israel on Birthright and was drawn to the culture and the land.

    Growing up under the thumb of Christianity, I found that even the simplest parts of my life were affected by the tenuous grip of the Bible and its people. I squirmed through invocations at sporting events (in Georgia, football is big, y’all), and I cried angry tears of disgust when a boyfriend dumped me for “being Jewish.” I found myself so alienated by the so-called “good word” that any inclinations by others toward the death grip of religion brought a gurgling fear to the pit of my stomach.

    I had shied away from Judaism, too, for its enveloping culture, but my Jewish grandmother’s pride brought me closer to some spiritual truth. She paid for me to attend Jew camp, where I was wooed by the curly amber tendrils of the boys in kippahs. But it was the smile on her face when I discussed Birthright that truly pushed me closer; for the first time, I felt her coaxing pull as she tried to lure me closer to a forged sense of spirituality. When I called her this High Holidays to inform her that I had decided to become a part of the faith, it was the undisguised pleasure in her voice that made even the long hours of services worthwhile.

    It was thus not the sputtering repetitions of the holy word that sparked my interest this High Holidays. It was, rather, the meditation of the universal truths — patience, virtue and growth — and the approval of my grandmother.

    In Judaism, the High Holidays are important in establishing the hopes and expectations for a new year, and it is convenient that the Jewish New Year coincides with the commencement of the quarter system. Freshman year is chocked full of poor life choices, from brazen introductions to blistered heels. In time, you find you have an established network of friends and peers; you have relationships and memories and burdens and lessons carried over from quarters prior; you have a foundation of knowledge of just what to expect; and you have the self-awareness to know just what your fragile GPA/roommate/liver can handle.

    Sure, the High Holidays are the holiest days of the year for Judaism. And those days are about repentance, atonement and a fresh start. But they also offer another chance to get things right. It’s a celebration of the truly good times and a consideration of those which could have played out better. It’s a way to analyze the relationships that fell apart and the friendships that fell to the wayside, a means to ask forgiveness and try to right the wrongs for the future. It is a test in patience (not merely due to the lengthy, occasional monotony of the services themselves) in the virtue of humanity, the knowledge and recognition that the things that are supposed to happen simply will. It is, in so many ways, relinquishing power not to the higher deity but to existence itself.

    I think there are important lessons rooted in the New Year that can benefit everyone this academic year. It’s not the overt religiosity and proclamation of devotion but rather the ideals of dedication, patience, honesty and self-satisfaction.

    For me, the New Year is to be filled with health, happiness and, of course, the hellishness of being a melodramatic adolescent. I cannot vow that I will not find myself at the mercy of the porcelain goddess, or that I will not choose sleep over journalism multiple times. But I can promise myself to take stock of the memories of the past, of the people I have loved and continue to love, and hope to G-d that I find a place for them in my new life.

    So, I suppose these High Holidays commemorate not only the start of a new year but also the one year anniversary of my holiest tryst with spirituality. Moreover, it’s the start of a new chapter in the search for my identity, and for ours collectively as we attempt to create our Northwestern experience, together and apart, one year at a time.
