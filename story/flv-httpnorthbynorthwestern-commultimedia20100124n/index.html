How to keep your New Year's resolutions without torturing yourself
By Abby Shure, Ngozi Ekeledo
3:55 p.m. Jan. 24 2010

    Get the latest Flash Player to see this player.

    Video by Ngozi Ekeledo.

    It’s been more than three weeks since New Year’s Day. Let’s face it. You’ve given up on your resolutions. You go for peanut butter and french fries instead of carrots and apples. You threw your workout log in the trash a week ago. Blomquist is foreign territory again.

    Why is it so hard to keep resolutions? You thought you were so good.

    And that might be the problem. You might think you’re too good. According to Loran Nordgren, a senior lecturer of management and organizations at the Kellogg School, people’s tendencies to “overestimate self-control capacity” makes it harder to stick with difficult resolutions.

    “You think you have greater ability to resist temptations … people have less control over temptation then they imagine,” he says.

    While not all resolutions deal with temptation, he says, “people so readily fail because they rely on willpower.” His research shows that people tend to think they can handle any temptation, so they take less precaution when they know they’re going to be around a vice, like alcohol or French fries.

    Nordgren published a study that dealt with four experiments over the past few years. The studies proved that in situations of arousal (like hunger), participants were more likely to understand their own potential for temptation. When participants weren’t able to have that same feeling of impulse (being full, for example), they believed they had a much higher threshold for self-control. Participants caved in experiments when they pushed themselves too far into temptation because they overestimated their self-control.

    Instead of starving yourself while trying to resist raiding the dining hall, “create barriers” between you and your vice to stick with a goal.

    Nordgren’s steps to success: think about when you’ll be most vulnerable, and then get creative so you’re not just depending on hope and willpower to make sure you don’t light up or sneak some chocolate.

    “You just have to try to buffer yourself,” he says.
