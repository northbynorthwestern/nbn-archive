No credit? Big problem.
By Abby Shure
2:07 p.m. April 8 2010

    Photo by Emily Chow / North by Northwestern
    Discover Student Card
    Perks: Up to 5% cash back, no annual fee, reasonable APR*, easy to manage online, no minimum income.
    Watch out for: Usual pitfalls of having a card, nothing special here.
    For: Pretty much anyone. It has a nice intro period, so you can ease into understanding how you’re going to use your card. Other than that, it’s pretty standard for cash back, and you’ll get money for spending around campus.

    Citi Forward Card for College Students
    Perks: Reward points, lower APR if you stay under your limit and pay on time, lots of bonus reward point opportunities, no annual fee.
    Watch out for: Bonuses related to how much money you spend – if you don’t have it, don’t spend it! Also, potentially higher APR than other cards.
    For: Responsible users. If you overspend and pay late, you’re going to pay for it with this card. If you’re good about using it, you’ll get rewards and lower rates, which makes it enticing if you can make sure you pay on time.

    Capital One Student Rewards Credit Card
    Perks: Up to 2% cash back, no annual fee
    Watch our for: Just general responsible spending, the usual APR and other fees.
    For: Someone who probably won’t use their card much. There aren’t a lot of frills here, so if you’re looking to get tons of help or rewards, this probably isn’t the card for you.

    Citi mtvU Platinum Select Visa Card for College Students
    Perks: Reward points for places you’ll spend money in Evanston, low APR to start, points twice a year for a good GPA (sorry, engineers!), points for paying on time and staying below the credit limit, no annual fee.
    Watch our for: A lot of the best perks are only for cardholders who qualify, so if this is your first card, you might not get all the awesome stuff.
    For: The MTV Generation who want a card very clearly aimed at them. This also has useful features and bonuses for being a good NU student.

    *APR, or “annual percentage rate,” is the cost of having credit as a yearly interest rate. When you have a card, you get charged a fraction of that rate every month as interest.

    Looking to pay for an off-campus apartment without your parents? Your landlord is going to check your credit. Thinking about buying a car? The dealership is definitely going to check your credit. Just trying to figure out how to pay for books, booze and Whole Foods? If you do it right, that’s the best way to build up good credit.

    Having a credit card in college is crucial to achieving financial independence after four years at NU, and being smart about using it will give you a clean credit score (and the possibility of buying the next car, house or flat-screen TV that needs to be financed).

    A good credit score indicates that a person has a good record -– it means that “you know how to borrow and repay,” according to Mitchell A. Petersen, the Glen Vasel Professor of Finance at Kellogg. It comes from paying off card balances on time each month.

    To get to that point, you’ll need to show that you’re financially independent by paying for something on your own, not by using your parents’ account.  As you build your credit in college, you’ll establish a longer record than you would have if you waited until graduation to start your line of personal credit, Kellogg professor Robert Korajczyk said.

    When you apply for a credit card, banks will be wary because you haven’t been able to show that you’re capable of being responsible with one yet. Without the credit score that comes from paying off a credit card, you’re basically “invisible” in terms of your personal credit; companies can’t see any of your financial history. Instead, they just see that you have no credit score at all. Without one, you won’t be able to get a car loan or mortgage.

    “You need to build credit now,” said Weinberg sophomore Katie Levin, who has had a credit card since she started college. She has a Visa with a college account because her mom explained to her that she needed to be responsible for herself.

    When you’re looking for the perfect card to build your credit rating, you’ll need to weigh the costs and benefits of different cards including late fees, interest rates and benefits like airline miles. As you look at costs and fees, keep in mind that it might be stuff that won’t affect you, like penalties for late payments (which, if you’re smart, you won’t make).

    “Pick a card for what you think you’ll be like,” then stick to it, Petersen advises. Deciding that you’ll always pay on time, for example, means that you can choose a card with a high late fee and other benefits because you ideally won’t get hit by the fine. Many companies offer students “college accounts” with helpful features like bill reminders and emails if your account hits a certain balance.

    If self-control is an issue, use a debit card for purchases so that the money is taken right away and there isn’t temptation to spend more than you immediately have. Your thought process should be, “How badly do I want those jeans, that McDonalds?” Petersen said. While a debit card won’t help build credit, it will keep you from overspending until you can control your purchases enough to use a credit card.

    Consumer debt has increased greatly in the past 20 years. People are willing to borrow for anything, like those $200 jeans — or even that $5 Happy Meal. Petersen predicts that the trend will swing back to people being more conservative with their credit purchases as people become more cognizant of what they’re doing and gain self-control.

    “You need to use it judiciously,” said Korajczyk. Try using it only for something specific, like textbooks, and then paying it off immediately, he said. As a student, “you need to remember that your first order of business is to study.”

    Having a card in college, then, becomes a “rude awakening to the adult world,” Levin said. “When you think of credit cards, you think of cool adults and frivolous spending,” she said. But in fact, “it’s a huge responsibility.”
