Why the iPad isn't worth it yet
By Brian Lange
3 p.m. Feb. 3 2010

    Last Wednesday, Apple CEO Steve Jobs stepped onstage in his trademark jeans and black turtleneck to reveal to the world Apple’s latest gadget — a tablet computer dubbed the iPad.

    With a 9.7 inch touchscreen encased in a smooth aluminum body, the iPad can most easily be described as an enlarged iPod Touch — it runs the latest version of the same software used on the iPod Touch and iPhone. It can run almost all the apps available for both of those devices, in addition to some new ones designed especially for the iPad. These include revamped versions of standards like iCalendar and Mail, as well as new apps like a specially designed version of the iWork suite and, most notably, iBooks — an app for buying and reading books like you might on Amazon’s Kindle or Barnes & Noble’s Nook.

    The devices comes in storage sizes from 16-64 GB, and some versions will allow you to connect to AT&T 3G wireless internet anywhere you can get service, for a monthly fee. The base model starts at $500, and advanced versions go up to 64GB of memory and 3G connectivity; these cost a hefty $829.

    First impressions of the device from the press and online community were very mixed. Josh Topolsky, editor of Engadget.com, asserted that “the case for the iPad has yet to be made”, while Larry Magid of CBS News said he was “underwhelmed.” Meanwhile David Pogue of the New York Times reacted against early negative opinions, reminding all the haters “how silly you all looked when you predicted the iPhone’s demise in that period before it went on sale.”

    On campus, opinions of the iPad were all over the board. McCormick sophomore Andrew Briggs watched the Apple presentation on liveblogs as it happened. “Watching Jobs present the iPad was nothing less than disappointing,” he said, citing the device’s use of the iPhone operating system instead of a full one like you might find on a laptop. “Right now, it’s a tremendously stupid device that, instead of perfecting a niche market and bringing it mainstream, digs itself into irrelevance by ultimately failing to be anything other than a novelty.”

    Others echoed his sentiment. “The biggest dealbreaker for me is multitasking,” said McCormick freshman Gursimran Singh. “My phone can do more multitasking than the iPad.” Other students mentioned its lack of a webcam and physical keyboard as letdowns, though Apple currently plans on releasing a keyboard accessory for the device.

    Not all Northwestern students had a negative opinion. “It’s a revolution by step,” said Bienen freshman Robinson Meyer. “The iPhone was the first step. We’re moving towards a world of closed but efficient computing. It will change the way we think of computers, eventually.”

    But there’s one thing most students agree on: Not too many seem eager to go pick an iPad up on launch day. “For any college student, spending $500 is not a small deal,” said Communication freshman Zach Sorrow. “I want one. But practically, I don’t think I would buy one.”

    Meyer wants to get one down the road. “After my summer job, it might make more sense,” he said. “But I’d like to eat spring quarter.”

    Northwestern students do have some ideas of how the iPad could be used in a college student’s life. Sorrow mentioned interactive textbooks: “I should be able to read my textbook, but also have hyperlinks in it.”

    As a Bienen student, Meyer saw a different use. “Music writing software. Unlike past computers, you could put it on a music stand and tinker around on the screen while you play. That would be the killer app for me.”

    It seems Apple and their army of app developers have a lot of work to do to convince NU students that the iPad is something they need. With each added function, however, that big price tag is going to start looking smaller and smaller.
