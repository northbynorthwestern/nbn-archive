Fall Out Boy isn't just for emo kids
By Abby Shure
4:41 p.m. Jan. 31 2010

    Pop Culture Confessional is a weekly column where our writers can divulge and indulge in their most deeply embarrassing cultural passion — and then tell you why it actually rocks. Everyone has a few dirty little secrets. Only the truth shall set us free.

    Fall Out Boy. Photo by Freedom To Marry on Flickr, licensed under the Creative Commons.

    I’m not emo, and it’s probably because I don’t scowl well. I also don’t wear band T-shirts, have extra piercings or tattoos or write my own terrible poetry. I wear a normal amount of eyeliner, keep my Facebook status superficial and enjoy bright colors.

    Also, I completely love Fall Out Boy.

    I listen to Fall Out Boy at the gym, hitting drum solos on the elliptical handles and air-guitaring in the middle of sets of crunches. The band pumps me up to actually get some homework done (sometimes), and it’s my go-to music if I need an emotional pick-me-up. FOB is always on, but only with headphones — it’s hard to seriously admit that I like such a cheesy pseudo-punk band. When they come on as part of a getting-ready playlist in my dorm, I act like it was an accident and quickly skip to the next song.

    It’s taken me a long time to rationalize my love for the band — I consider myself something of a music connoisseur, like every other kid who liked Phoenix before that Cadillac commercial. My playlists mix Matt and Kim with Passion Pit and The Hives, so it seems like I have no business dealing with Fall Out Boy’s kind of pop-punk crap. But it’s some of my favorite so-called crap. Even though I’ve never been to an FOB concert, I’m just as dedicated as any die-hard fan. I have all of their albums and most of their remixes, and I’m not ashamed to tell it like it is: Fall Out Boy is a pop-punk band and they rock.

    Their music is well-composed and interesting. They started with a more classic rock/punk base, and then developed using new styles of composition and instrumentation. If you listen to all of their stuff, from their earliest album Take This To Your Grave to Folie à Deux, you can hear them grow and improve as a band, thanks to the genius of singer/composer/rythym guitarist Patrick Stump.

    Pete Wentz, their bassist and lyricist, is actually clever. He uses irony and puns, but he also writes stuff that’s not over my head or intentionally obscure. It’s easy to understand what he means (although sometimes slightly tricky because of all the wordplay) and I can definitely relate. FOB exemplifies why rock music is so good — the guitar and drums make your heart pound when your speakers are on full volume, and the lyrics you’re screaming are just about boys and girls and having a good time. It’s what makes music compelling, and Fall Out Boy has the perfect combination of self-aware attitude and killer rock to make them addicting.

    FOB started a genre, spawning imitators like Panic! At the Disco and All Time Low and influencing countless pop-punk tracks from other artists like My Chemical Romance and The Cab. They did it first, and they did it best — for every hit single, there are so many other amazing tracks that get no recognition. Gems like “Hum Hallelujah” and “She’s My Winona” shine with woah-oh choruses and heavy pop-punk guitar. They exemplify what’s awesome about the band: they are legitimately fun to listen to.

    Fall Out Boy isn’t just for punks or kids who scribble sad faces on to their wrists with permanent marker, but for anyone who’s ready to feel like a rock star. Try it out, into your hairbrush/water bottle/cell phone: woahhhhhhh oh. Trust me, you can get used to this.
