Expressing yourself in more than 140 characters
By Brian Lange
2:44 p.m. May 11 2010

    Ever feel limited by the paltry 140 characters of a tweet? Constrained by the short form of “status updates” that we’ve all grown accustomed to? Oftentimes social networks aren’t enough to share our thoughts, dreams and complaints with the world. For those times when you can’t cram what you want to get off your chest into a tiny box, blogging may be the antidote.

    But where to start? There are lots of free services online each with unique strengths and weaknesses. Here’s the breakdown:

    Screenshot from the Wordpress Plugin Directory.

    Wordpress.com
    Wordpress is an established, popular system for publications all over the net (like North By Northwestern) to manage and publish articles, and Wordpress.com is their personal blogging service. It’s free to sign up and offers lots of robust features like sleek, modern themes with customizable widgets, comments with moderation, a statistics system and 3GB of storage for pictures and other multimedia. If you’re looking for a traditional blog with powerful features, Wordpress is a safe bet, though the interface can be a little overwhelming.

    Pros: Good themes, lots of powerful features, customizable sidebar widgets.
    Cons: Not the simplest to use, some premium features cost extra.
    Famous example: http://stylenews.peoplestylewatch.com/

    Screenshot from Tumblr’s Theme Garden.

    Tumblr
    Tumblr seeks to fill the gap in between long, ranting blog posts and micro tweets and statuses. It’s very simple to set up, has a ton of gorgeous themes and handles pretty much anything you want to post on it, from simple text to photos, links, quotes and audio and video recordings. Tumblr also features strong social network support — it can pull in your feeds from all over the net and publish your posts to Facebook and Twitter too.

    Pros: Strong social and multimedia support, easy to use, great themes.
    Cons: Lacks more robust features like traditional comments, stat tracking.
    Famous example: http://www.latfh.com/

    Screenshot taken from Blogger’s dashboard.

    Blogger
    Blogger is Google’s blogging platform, which, like Wordpress, follows the more traditional route. It features the standard features like comments and customizable layouts, but Blogger’s real strength is its integration with Google AdWords to help bloggers monetize. If you’ve got readers, Blogger tries to make it as easy as possible to place some ads and make money. However, while everyone loves making dough, Blogger’s interface and themes feel a little outdated, and it doesn’t have the same ease of service use like Tumblr and Posterous.

    Pros: Easy monetization, standard blog features
    Cons: Themes and interface could use a facelift, not the easiest to use.
    Famous example: http://postsecret.blogspot.com/

    Screenshot taken from TypePad Micro’s dashboard.

    TypePad Micro
    Made by the people who developed the popular MovableType platform, TypePad Micro is free and focuses on shorter posts and multimedia, similar to Tumblr. In comparison to Tumblr, Typepad offers a few more features like stats tracking and comments, but doesn’t quite match up in the social connectivity department and only offers a couple themes that are mildly customizable.

    Pros: Easy to post to, simple system with a few extra features
    Cons: Very limited themes, social integration could be better.
    Famous example: http://opheliadog.typepad.com/microdogging/

    Screenshot from Posterous’ social autopost page.

    Posterous
    Posterous takes a different approach to blogging — rather than go to the website to post like most services, simply send an e-mail containing text, photos, or whatever else you want to post to post@posterous.com. The upside of this is that it’s simple and can be done from any device that does e-mail — phone, iPod Touch, etc. Granted, other services like Tumblr and TypePad Micro have similar functionality, but don’t match Posterous’ social network integration and control. For example, if you want to post a video on your Facebook but not your YouTube, simply send it to facebook@posterous.com and that’s where it’ll end up. Posterous also features comments, but doesn’t have the statistical tracking features that Wordpress and TypePad Micro have.

    Pros: Dead simple, tons of social network support, easy to post to from anywhere.
    Cons: Not conducive to longer posts or more complicated pages. Themes are nice but limited.
    Famous example: http://tedfellows.posterous.com/
