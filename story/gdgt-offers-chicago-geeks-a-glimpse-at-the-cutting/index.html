Gdgt offers Chicago geeks a glimpse at the cutting edge
By Brian Lange
4:05 p.m. May 13 2010

    The line to get in wrapped around the corner Wednesday night at Gallery 233 in Chicago’s near north side. Hundreds of gadget geeks from Indianapolis, Milwaukee and all corners of Chicagoland descended on “gdgt live” for a glimpse at the near future in technology. And Northwestern, the future looks bright.

    Boxee was one of 18 companies who attended the live event to show off their wares, and while there were plenty of shiny, expensive gadgets to play with on the show floor, Boxee offered something much more affordable.

    Photo by the author / North by Northwestern.

    Boxee is free software for Windows, Mac, Linux and Apple TV that makes it easy to watch content from the internet using a computer and a remote.

    “[Students] who have a TV in their room that they don’t wanna pay for cable for, can use Boxee, hook up that laptop that I know they have with a $10 cable, and sit back on their bed or their couch and surf the web in a way that isn’t in a browser, it’s actually in a way that’s optimized for their TV and for a remote,” Andrew Kippen, vice president of marketing for Boxee said.

    Boxee pulls in content from online sources like Netflix, Pandora, Facebook, NBC and more.

    “We’ve got over 300 different applications, plus [students] can play everything they’ve got on their hard drive,” Kippen said.

    The real buzz at gdgt, however, was around the latest smartphones, with the lines at HTC, Windows Phone, and Kin rivaled only by the line at the bar.

    Windows Phone 7 is Microsoft’s latest attempt at the smartphone market, and while the first Windows Phone 7 phones won’t come out until the holiday season, the unit they were showing off at gdgt already looked promising.

    JP Wollersheim, a group project manager for Windows Phone showed me around the unique features of the new phones. One unique feature is People, a sort of enhanced contact book which pulls in all your contacts from Facebook and other sources as well as their Facebook and Twitter feeds, and organizes them by person.

    “You don’t have to go into a Facebook App, a Twitter App, all these different apps. It’s all integrated right here in one common view, ” Wollersheim said.

    “It’s the same sort of thing for Pictures,” Wollersheim continued. The Pictures panel holds not only the photos on your phone, but all your Facebook photos and a feed of the latest pictures from your Facebook friends. Facebook stalkers, this is your phone.

    For addicts fans of social networks that don’t want a smartphone, Microsoft showed off Kin, a line of not-quite-smart phones that launched yesterday on Verizon and were shown for the first time in public at gdgt. “It’s aimed at a younger, socially connected generation,” Patrick Neighborn, a representative for Kin said.

    “Kin’s really all about sharing. So if I wanna share something on Kin, I just drag it to the Spot,” Neighborn said, dragging a picture to a dot at the bottom of the screen. “And then I drag my friends to the Spot, and I can send it to them.”

    Another particularly useful feature for those of us who are more clumsy with our electronics is Kin Studio. “It backs up to Kin Studio, so if you lose your phone you don’t lose your stuff. You never have to like, plug in a USB cable or send emails to yourself. It just does it,” Neighborn said.

    Kin wasn’t the only company with the clumsy phone user in mind at the event. Igor Muravyov, chief marketing officer for Wrapsol, showed off his product, a thin plastic film you can put on smartphones and other gadgets.

    “It gives you full drop protection, knock or scratch protection, so you can take an iPhone, and you can drop it on concrete like that,” Murayov said, dropping a seemingly naked iPhone 5 feet onto the concrete floor. “And there’s absolutely no damage. So it’s as strong as a traditional case but you still get the aesthetic look of a traditional iPhone.”

    So if there’s one thing to be learned from gdgt, it’s that whether you’re a Hulu fanatic, a phone abuser, or someone who’s got a little Facebook problem, the future of gadgetry’s got you covered.
