School of Phish
By Coco Keevan
9:34 p.m. Nov. 18 2010

    Photo by Ariana Bacle / North by Northwestern

    In what was to be his last year at Northwestern, Ed Krystosek found himself at an existential crossroad. His financial situation “went kaput in a cataclysmic way,” leaving him unrecognized as a financial independent and locked out of the American education system. Out of options, Krystosek took a break, gathered his bags and followed his heart across country.

    Now a 37-year-old, second-time Northwestern undergrad, Krystosek spent nearly 10 of the past 15 years travelling by plane, bus, train and boat to follow his favorite band, Phish. Some of Phish’s followers are known as kids or kidz. The name suits Krystosek perfectly.

    “When I’m at their shows, I’m 40 feet wide by 60 feet long,” Krystosek says. “[It’s] ecstatic dancing on a grand scale. I’ve never felt anything else that does that for me or that does it to as many beautiful people that I love to be surrounded by as they do.”

    Phish saturates Krystosek’s memories. He remembers each show as a distinct movement in body and soul. Krystosek has attended 285 Phish concerts, starting with a 1993 show at Chicago’s Aragon Ballroom when he was 19. Those concerts cost Krystosek, of course, so he found a way to work where he played.

    “My primary moneymaker was selling bottled water,” Krystosek says. “I’d stand directly in front of the gate to the venue and remind everybody passing me that water inside was $3 a bottle. You can make 600 bottles of water disappear in the hour before the concert.”

    When the band split in 2004, Krystosek focused on theater, working as an actor, producer and, most recently, director in his hometown of Chicago. He maintains a position as an artistic associate at The Artistic Home, an actor-centric company for working professionals. But when Phish reunited five years later and went on tour, Krystosek resumed his nearly rabid following.

    “When they called it quits, I called it quits with that kind of lifestyle for five years,” he says. “When they got back on the road, I was right back out on the road.”

    Having returned last fall, Krystosek is now in his truly final year. He’s determined to complete his degree and capture the theater community with “Thank You, Everything,” a play he penned for class based on his time on the road. The show premieres this winter, produced by Northwestern’s Vertigo productions.

    “In terms of playwriting, whether you like it or not, with age comes experience,” says Communication senior Aaron Eisenberg, who took a playwriting course with Krystosek. “He’s, like, 15 years more well-read than anyone in the class. He’s older than the teacher. He does bring in this kind of experience and this breadth of knowledge that a lot of us don’t have.”

    Northwestern isn’t identical to the campus Krystosek first joined in 1991 — back then Norris was still a bar and the Wildcats played at Dyche Stadium. But for the most part, Krystosek still feels like a kid.

    “There was a part of me that was prepared to be that weird old man on campus,” Krystosek admits. “But I’m generally treated as a peer by everyone — just another student.”

    A previous version of this article stated that Phish’s followers are known as kids. Wording has been changed to more accurately reflect the scope of the term. North by Northwestern regrets the error.
